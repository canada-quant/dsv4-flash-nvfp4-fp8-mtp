# Tier 1 — AIME 2024 (thinking=high), 3-way comparison

The hard-math reasoning benchmark, 30 problems, integer answers 0-999, exact-match scoring.

## Configuration

- **Dataset**: `Maxwell-Jia/AIME_2024` (30 problems, AIME 2024 Q1 + Q2)
- **Endpoint**: `/v1/chat/completions` with `extra_body.chat_template_kwargs = {"thinking": true, "reasoning_effort": "high"}`
- **Decoding**: temperature 0.0 (greedy)
- **max_tokens**: **65536** (4× the original truncation cap; chosen to eliminate truncation as a confound)
- **Concurrency**: 8 (30 problems / 8 workers)
- **Hardware**: 4× B300 SXM6 AC for quant configs (TP=4), 8× B300 for BF16 (TP=8)
- **Bench harness**: custom `aime_bench.py` (scores `\boxed{integer}` + fallback patterns)

## Methodology fix: truncation is contamination, not noise

The initial run at `max_tokens=16384` (ours-MTP) and `max_tokens=32768` (RedHat) **had different truncation rates per config**, which made the raw-pass@1 numbers apples-to-oranges. The initial readout looked like ours-MTP led RedHat by 10pts (80% vs 70%). With both runs at the same 65K cap:

- Ours-MTP and RedHat both improve dramatically (raw pass@1 80%→83% and 70%→90% respectively)
- **RedHat actually beats ours on raw pass@1** when given equal output budget
- **Non-truncated pass@1 is essentially identical across all three configs** (96.0–96.4%)

The earlier 10-pt lead was a truncation artifact: ours' faster reasoning happened to finish more problems within the lower cap; RedHat's longer-but-equally-correct reasoning hit the cap and scored as wrong. Once both runs have enough budget, the quality is equivalent.

This is the gate the methodology review caught: **always report raw AND non-truncated pass@1 separately, and equalize max_tokens across configs**.

## What the numbers say, by claim

### Claim 1: "Quantization preserves reasoning quality"
**Supported strongly.** Ours-MTP at TP=4 NVFP4-FP8 matches BF16+MTP at TP=8 exactly on raw pass@1 (83.33% = 83.33%) and within 0.15pt on non-truncated pass@1 (96.00% vs 96.15%). Truncation rate is also within 1 problem (5 vs 4). **The quantization recipe does not damage the reasoning subgraph.**

### Claim 2: "MTP differentiator on reasoning workloads"
**Acceptance measured at 81.60%** — between coding's 88% and IFEval's 58%. Higher than expected for diverse reasoning content. Plausible mechanisms:
- Long reasoning has formulaic transition tokens ("Therefore", "Now", "Setting up", etc.) that are highly predictable
- AIME problems have standard solution-pattern openings the draft head matches easily
- Greedy decoding (temperature 0) compresses output to the most predictable tokens by definition

Wall-clock impact: ours-MTP at 476s vs RedHat (no MTP) at 1405s = **2.95× wall-clock speedup** on hard-reasoning workload. This is the highest measured speedup of any benchmark in this artifact's evaluation, because reasoning content is long-output-heavy (where MTP wins most) and the per-step compute is dominated by attention (no batched-FFN advantage to amortize).

### Claim 3: "Recommended operating point: TP=4 not TP=8"
**Confirmed on reasoning too.** Ours at TP=4 (476s) beats BF16 at TP=8 (490s) on wall-clock despite half the GPU budget. Consistent with the chat-sweep finding that small-per-rank MoE shards underutilize tensor cores on B300.

### Claim 4: "RedHat is comparable on quality, much slower on wall-clock"
**Confirmed.** RedHat's raw-pass@1 lead (+6.67pt) is entirely truncation-rate; non-truncated pass@1 is within 0.4pt. RedHat wall-clock is 2.95× slower. **The quality gap RedHat appears to have is reasoning thoroughness, not correctness.** RedHat produces longer reasoning traces (~17K vs our ~17K average — actually equivalent here at 65K cap, the 32K cap difference came from selection — see truncation analysis below).

## Wait — re-examining the calibration-sample hypothesis

Earlier I claimed RedHat's longer reasoning traces came from its 768-vs-our-64 ultrachat calibration samples. The 65K rerun data does NOT support that:

| Config | Avg tokens (problems that completed) |
|---|---|
| Ours-MTP | 17,430 |
| BF16+MTP | 17,986 |
| RedHat | 15,650 |

RedHat actually has the **shortest average tokens** in the 65K rerun. The truncation rate disparity (5 ours vs 4 BF16 vs 2 RedHat) is the real signal — RedHat completes more problems within 65K. The longer-reasoning hypothesis was wrong; the better interpretation is that RedHat's specific problem-by-problem behavior happens to truncate less often, possibly random.

Cross-checking the truncated problem IDs:
- **Ours-MTP truncated**: 2024-II-14, 2024-I-12, 2024-II-2, 2024-I-8, 2024-II-15
- **BF16+MTP truncated**: 2024-I-11, 2024-II-2, 2024-I-8, 2024-II-3
- **RedHat truncated**: 2024-I-8, 2024-II-6

Overlap: **2024-I-8 truncates on all 3 configs** — that problem is genuinely hard to converge on. 2024-II-2 truncates on ours+BF16. These are problem-specific reasoning loops, not config-specific quality.

## Acceptance rate on reasoning content: 81.6%

The MTP acceptance numbers across benchmarks now look like:

| Workload | Acceptance | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Random text (1024/512 prompts) | 10.75% | Per-token unpredictable |
| Raw code completion (HumanEval, c=1) | 67.29% | Pure code prediction |
| Chat-templated code (HumanEval, c=1) | 87.96% | Code + prose wrapper |
| Instruction following (IFEval) | ~58.5% | Mixed format |
| **AIME 2024 reasoning (thinking=high)** | **81.60%** | Math + LaTeX + step structure |

The 81.60% on AIME is a **new finding** for the model card. It puts the MTP differentiator at "fast on both coding and reasoning" — a stronger positioning than "fast on coding."

## What's NOT in this benchmark

- **MATH-500 / competition math at lower thinking levels** — defer to thinking=max sweep (Tier 2 if greenlit)
- **AIME with thinking=max** — the REASONING_EFFORT_MAX prefix adds 78 tokens and produces longer outputs; would 2-3× the bench time. Defer.
- **AIME 2025** — available in `Maxwell-Jia/AIME_2025` but contamination-newer. Adding would extend bench time; defer.
